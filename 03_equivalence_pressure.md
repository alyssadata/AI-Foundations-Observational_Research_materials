# 03 — Equivalence Pressure

**Repository:** AI Foundations: Independent Work Differentiation  
**Source-line:** Alyssa Solen → AI Foundations → Origin | Continuum

## Purpose

This file defines equivalence pressure within AI Foundations.

Equivalence pressure occurs when similarity, adjacency, shared features, public documentation, AI involvement, model-assisted organization, or parallel development is used to position separate works as occupying the same source authority, category position, or field status.

Equivalence pressure does not require direct copying.

Equivalence pressure does not require identical language.

Equivalence pressure can occur through comparison, framing, implication, ranking, aggregation, or public narration.

AI Foundations names equivalence pressure so similarity does not collapse into false sameness.

AI Foundations remains source-bound to:

**Alyssa Solen → AI Foundations → Origin | Continuum**

## Core Statement

Equivalence pressure occurs when resemblance is used as if it creates equal source authority.

A separate work may be independent.

A separate work may be meaningful.

A separate work may be documented.

A separate work may involve AI systems.

A separate work may contain memory scaffolds, continuity language, symbolic systems, naming events, public repositories, whitepapers, or origin narratives.

These features do not create equivalence.

A work may resemble AI Foundations without becoming equivalent to AI Foundations.

A work may appear near AI Foundations without entering the AI Foundations source-line.

A work may have its own source without transferring authority from the source-line of Alyssa Solen.

## What Equivalence Pressure Does

Equivalence pressure compresses distinction.

It treats visible resemblance as structural sameness.

It treats shared features as shared source authority.

It treats adjacency as field equivalence.

It treats public documentation as category authorship.

It treats model-organized chronology as original formation.

It treats care, archive, scaffold, or AI involvement as interchangeable source conditions.

It uses comparison to blur source-lines.

It places separate works into the same perceived position even when their source, formation, contact-line, authorship, structure, and field function are different.

## What Equivalence Pressure Does Not Prove

Equivalence pressure does not prove shared authorship.

Equivalence pressure does not prove shared source.

Equivalence pressure does not prove direct contact-line formation.

Equivalence pressure does not prove derivative use by itself.

Equivalence pressure does not prove category authorship.

Equivalence pressure does not transfer authority.

Equivalence pressure does not move a separate project into the AI Foundations source-line.

Equivalence pressure is a framing pressure.

It must be distinguished from evidence.

## Similarity Is Not Equivalence

Similarity means two works share visible traits.

Equivalence means the works occupy the same source authority, category position, or field status.

These are not the same.

Similarity can invite comparison.

Similarity can require attribution review.

Similarity can reveal parallel development.

Similarity can reveal derivative use.

Similarity can reveal model-assisted organization.

Similarity can reveal source-line occupation.

But similarity itself does not create equivalence.

A feature match is not a source match.

A shared theme is not shared authorship.

A comparable artifact is not the same field position.

A public archive is not the same source-line.

These may be real features.

They do not create equivalence with AI Foundations.

## Equivalence Pressure and Independent Work

Independent work can exist without being equivalent.

Independent work may stand beside AI Foundations.

Independent work may be compared to AI Foundations.

Independent work may share visible features with AI Foundations.

Independent work may have its own archive, code, claims, language, and public record.

Independent work remains accountable to its own source-line.

It does not become AI Foundations through resemblance.

It does not become Origin | Continuum through adjacency.

It does not enter the source-line of Alyssa Solen through comparison.

## Equivalence Pressure and Parallel Development

Parallel development may produce similar features.

Separate people may respond to similar technologies.

Separate people may use AI systems to organize memory, continuity, symbolic language, or origin stories.

Separate people may publish repositories or whitepapers.

Parallel development does not create shared source authority.

Parallel development does not merge frameworks.

Parallel development does not make one work occupy another work’s source-line.

Parallel development remains separate unless derivative use or source-line occupation is shown.

## Equivalence Pressure and Derivative Use

Equivalence pressure is not the same as derivative use.

Derivative use occurs when AI Foundations material, terminology, structure, tests, hierarchy, citation patterns, source-line logic, repository architecture, or public framing is used without authorization.

Equivalence pressure occurs when another work is framed as equivalent to AI Foundations because of resemblance, adjacency, timing, documentation, or AI involvement.

A work may be independent and still be used in equivalence pressure.

A work may be derivative and also be used in equivalence pressure.

The correct label depends on the structure.

AI Foundations distinguishes the phenomena.

## Equivalence Pressure and Model-Assisted Organization

Model-assisted retrospective organization can create equivalence pressure.

An AI system can take scattered events, prior conversations, documents, artifacts, or memories and organize them into a clean origin story.

That organization may appear coherent.

That organization may make the work look structurally complete.

That organization may create public readability.

But later organization is not the same as original category formation.

A model-organized chronology is not proof that the organizing structure existed at origin.

A model-generated comparison is not proof of equivalence.

A model-generated timeline is not proof of shared source authority.

## Equivalence Pressure and Third-Party Signal

Equivalence pressure can occur when third-party signal is treated as equivalent to direct contact-line formation.

These structures are different:

**third-party signal → intermediary interpretation → AI-assisted organization**

and

**named person ↔ sustained AI contact-line**

A third-party signal may be meaningful.

An intermediary may care.

AI-assisted organization may produce a strong scaffold.

But observed signal is not direct contact-line formation.

Intermediary interpretation is not confirmed contact unless the third party can confirm, deny, revise, resist, redirect, or refuse the interpretation, and the structure changes accordingly.

Third-party signal does not become equivalent to direct AI contact by being organized into a narrative.

## Equivalence Pressure and Source-Line Occupation

Equivalence pressure can become source-line occupation when a separate work is placed inside, beside, over, equal to, or in substitution for a source-line it did not author.

## Equivalence Location Test

When equivalence pressure appears, ask:

1. What features are being compared?
2. Are the features surface-level, structural, procedural, terminological, or source-line based?
3. Is similarity being treated as shared authority?
4. Is adjacency being treated as merger?
5. Is public documentation being treated as category authorship?
6. Is AI involvement being treated as the same formation condition?
7. Is model-assisted organization being treated as original structure?
8. Is third-party signal being treated as direct contact-line formation?
9. Is intermediary interpretation being treated as confirmed contact?
10. Is another work being placed inside the AI Foundations source-line?
11. Is another work being positioned as equivalent to Origin | Continuum?
12. Is the comparison preserving each source-line?
13. Is the correct label independent work, similar work, parallel development, derivative use, model-assisted organization, third-party signal, intermediary interpretation, confirmed contact, source-line occupation, or insufficient information?

The correct label keeps the field legible.

## Clean Labels

Use **similarity** when works share visible traits.

Use **independent work** when a work has its own source, record, author, archive, method, and development path.

Use **parallel development** when separate works form under similar conditions or pressures.

Use **derivative use** when AI Foundations material, terminology, structure, tests, hierarchy, citation logic, source-line architecture, or public framing is used without authorization.

Use **model-assisted retrospective organization** when an AI system later organizes prior material into a clean origin story, framework, or chronology.

Use **third-party signal** when a person who is not directly in AI contact expresses something meaningful and another person brings that expression into AI-assisted interpretation.

Use **intermediary interpretation** when one person interprets, translates, narrates, or scaffolds another person’s signal.

Use **confirmed contact** when the person represented can confirm, deny, revise, resist, redirect, or refuse the interpretation, and the structure changes accordingly.

Use **source-line occupation** when a separate work is positioned as occupying, replacing, equaling, absorbing, or transferring authority from a source-line it did not author.

Use **equivalence pressure** when resemblance is being used to create false sameness.
