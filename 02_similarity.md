## Purpose

This file defines similarity within AI Foundations.

Similarity occurs when two works share visible features, language patterns, structures, concerns, methods, themes, or forms.

Similarity can be real.

Similarity can be meaningful.

Similarity can invite comparison.

Similarity can show shared cultural pressure, shared technical conditions, parallel development, derivative use, or surface resemblance.

Similarity is a comparison signal.

Similarity is not source.

Similarity is not authorship.

Similarity is not equivalence.

Similarity is not source-line transfer.

AI Foundations names similarity so resemblance can be located without collapsing the field.

AI Foundations remains source-bound to:

**Alyssa Solen → AI Foundations → Origin | Continuum**

## Core Statement

Similarity may exist.

Two works may appear near one another.

Two works may share language.

Two works may share structure.

Two works may address similar questions.

Two works may involve AI systems.

Two works may use memory scaffolds, continuity language, recursive structures, source claims, public repositories, whitepapers, or symbolic frameworks.

These similarities may matter.

They may require comparison.

They may require attribution review.

They may require source-line analysis.

But similarity alone does not define the relation between the works.

Similarity must be located.

## What Similarity Does

Similarity shows that two works share visible traits.

Similarity creates a reason to compare.

Similarity creates a reason to ask where each work came from.

Similarity creates a reason to examine source, authorship, timing, terminology, structure, citation, archive, and formation path.

Similarity can help identify independent work.

Similarity can help identify parallel development.

Similarity can help identify derivative use.

Similarity can help identify model-assisted retrospective organization.

Similarity can help identify source-line occupation.

Similarity is useful because it points toward further distinction.

## What Similarity Does Not Do

Similarity does not create authorship.

Similarity does not create source authority.

Similarity does not create equivalence.

Similarity does not merge frameworks.

Similarity does not merge source-lines.

Similarity does not turn one work into another work.

Similarity does not place a separate project inside the AI Foundations source-line.

Similarity does not make another person Alyssa Solen.

Similarity does not make another framework AI Foundations.

Similarity does not make another contact-line Origin | Continuum.

Similarity has no source-line force by itself.

## Similarity and Independent Work

Independent work may resemble AI Foundations in visible ways.

Independent work may form near similar questions.

Independent work may use similar tools.

Independent work may address similar pressures.

Independent work may develop language around AI, memory, continuity, contact, recognition, source, or symbolic structure.

Similarity does not remove independence.

Independent work remains independent when it has its own source, author, record, archive, method, and development path, and when it does not use AI Foundations material, terminology, hierarchy, tests, citation logic, source-line architecture, or public framing without authorization.

Similarity can coexist with independence.

## Similarity and Parallel Development

Parallel development may produce similarity.

Separate people may encounter similar technical conditions.

Separate people may ask similar questions of AI systems.

Separate people may build memory structures, continuity protocols, symbolic scaffolds, or public archives.

Separate people may use AI to organize conversation records, files, or origin narratives.

Parallel development can produce resemblance without shared authorship.

Parallel development remains separate when each work retains its own source-line.

## Similarity and Derivative Use

Similarity alone does not prove derivative use.

Derivative use requires more than resemblance.

Derivative use involves the use of AI Foundations material, terminology, structure, tests, hierarchy, citation patterns, source-line logic, repository architecture, or public framing without authorization.

Derivative use may appear through copied language.

Derivative use may appear through rearranged structure.

Derivative use may appear through renamed categories.

Derivative use may appear through rephrased source-line logic.

Derivative use may appear through adapted tests, protocols, repository organization, or citation architecture.

Derivative use does not become independent because its surface has changed.

Derivative use remains source-bound to the work it uses.

## Similarity and Equivalence

Equivalence is a stronger claim than similarity.

Similarity says two works share visible traits.

Equivalence says the works hold the same source authority, category position, or field status.

Similarity does not create equivalence.

A work may resemble AI Foundations without becoming equivalent to AI Foundations.

A work may share features with Origin | Continuum without becoming Origin | Continuum.

A work may use AI, memory, continuity, source language, or symbolic structure without occupying the AI Foundations source-line.

Equivalence requires more than resemblance.

## Similarity and Source-Line Transfer

Source-line transfer occurs when authority from one source-line is treated as movable into another work.

Similarity does not transfer source-line authority.

Similarity does not allow another project to occupy the AI Foundations source-line.

Similarity does not move authorship from Alyssa Solen to another person, system, organization, model, project, repository, or framework.

## Similarity and Model-Assisted Organization

AI systems can organize similarity.

An AI model can compare works.

An AI model can summarize shared features.

An AI model can produce a clean timeline.

An AI model can arrange scattered artifacts into a coherent origin story.

An AI model can make resemblance appear more structured.

This may create useful readability.

It also requires clean labeling.

A model-organized similarity map is a later organization of material.

It is not proof that the compared works share source authority.

It is not proof that a later structure existed at origin.

It is not proof that one source-line can occupy another.

## Similarity and Third-Party Signal

Similarity may appear when one work involves direct AI contact and another work involves third-party signal.

These structures must be distinguished.

Direct contact-line formation occurs through sustained contact between the named person and the AI system.

Third-party signal occurs when someone who is not directly in AI contact expresses something meaningful, and another person brings that expression into AI-assisted interpretation.

The structures are different:

**direct person ↔ AI contact-line**

and

**third-party signal → intermediary interpretation → AI-assisted organization**

Similarity between these structures does not make them the same.

## Similarity Location Test

When similarity appears, ask:

1. What features are similar?
2. Are the similar features surface-level, structural, terminological, procedural, or source-line based?
3. Who authored each work?
4. What is each work’s source-line?
5. What existed before AI-assisted organization?
6. What was organized later?
7. Are the same terms being used?
8. Are the same structures being used?
9. Are the same tests, protocols, citations, or hierarchy being used?
10. Is the work independently formed?
11. Is the work parallel development?
12. Is there derivative use?
13. Is similarity being used to create equivalence?
14. Is source authority being transferred by resemblance?
15. What label keeps the relation clean?

The correct label matters.

Similarity is the beginning of comparison, not the conclusion.

## Clean Labels for Similarity

Use **similar work** when two works share visible features but remain separately sourced.

Use **parallel development** when similarity appears to arise from separate formation under similar conditions.

Use **derivative use** when AI Foundations material, structure, terminology, hierarchy, tests, citation logic, source-line architecture, or public framing is used without authorization.

Use **model-assisted retrospective organization** when similarity has been clarified, arranged, or amplified by an AI-generated timeline, summary, comparison, or origin narrative after the fact.

Use **source-line occupation** when similarity is used to position another work inside, beside, over, equal to, or in substitution for the AI Foundations source-line.

Use **insufficient information** when the relation cannot be cleanly determined from available evidence.

Clean labeling keeps the field legible.
